2. Air Compressors 101

What Is an Air Compressor?

An air compressor converts power (electric or gas) into pressurized air stored in a tank. That pressurized air then powers pneumatic tools, CNC machines, paint booths, packaging lines — basically any industrial operation that needs a blast of controlled airflow.

Think of it as the fourth utility in a manufacturing facility — right after electricity, water, and gas. Most shops can't operate without it.

Two Main Types You'll Encounter

Piston / Reciprocating

  • Like a car engine — pistons pump up and down to compress air
  • Louder, runs in start/stop cycles
  • Lower upfront cost
  • Best for: small shops, intermittent use
  • Common competitor: old Ingersoll Rand pistons (you'll hear this a lot)

Rotary Screw

  • Two helical screws spin together continuously to compress air
  • Quieter, runs continuously
  • Higher upfront cost, but far more efficient long-term
  • Best for: CNC shops, production floors, anything running 8+ hrs/day
  • This is our primary product line

3. Accessories: Dryers, Filters, OWS & Drains

A compressor alone isn't a complete system. Compressed air comes out hot, wet, and dirty. Before it hits the machines, it needs to be dried, filtered, and the condensate (dirty water) needs to be disposed of legally. That's where accessories come in.

The Complete Air System
Compressor → Air Dryer → Filters → Machines
↓ condensate from dryer ↓
Oil-Water Separator → Drain → Legal Disposal

Air Dryers

Compressed air generates moisture. If wet air hits CNC machines, you get rust, tool wear, and bad surface finishes. Dryers remove that moisture.

Refrigerated Dryers

This is what you recommend 90% of the time.

  • Cools air to 38°F dew point
  • Removes most moisture effectively
  • Lower cost than desiccant
  • Perfect for: general CNC, fab shops, auto body, most manufacturing
CFM RatingDew PointPairs With
50 CFM38°F5-10 HP compressors
100 CFM38°F25 HP compressors
250 CFM38°F30+ HP / multi-unit

Desiccant Dryers

Only recommend when the application specifically demands it.

  • Achieves -40°F dew point (ultra-dry air)
  • More expensive to buy and operate
  • Required for: pharmaceutical, food & beverage, laser cutting, paint finishing, some aerospace
CFM RatingDew PointUse Case
50 CFM-40°FPharma, food processing
100 CFM-40°FLaser cutting, clean rooms
Alex’s Rule — Don’t Default to Desiccant

From Alex (Nucleus Launch call): “Most often you can get away with a refrigerated dryer and save them on cost. If you're the only one quoting a desiccant dryer and everybody else has refrigerated, that could price you out.”

Always ask: “What are you using your compressed air for specifically?” If they say general CNC machining, go refrigerated. Only go desiccant if they mention laser cutting, clean rooms, food/pharma, or specific manufacturer requirements for ultra-dry air.

Filters

Filters remove particles, oil aerosol, and vapor from compressed air. There are three main types, and they're installed in sequence:

Filter TypeWhat It RemovesWhen to Recommend
Particulate Filter Dust, rust, pipe scale (solid particles) Every system — this is the baseline
Coalescing Filter Oil aerosol and fine mist Any oil-lubricated compressor (which is all of ours)
Activated Carbon Filter Oil vapor and odor Food, pharma, breathing air, paint finishing
Ask Paul — Filter Sizing

Filter sizing depends on the specific system. Paul and Alex can help determine the right filter arrangement and models. Your job on the call is to ask what filtration they currently have — this tells us a lot about their setup and what they might need.

Oil-Water Separators (OWS)

When air is compressed, moisture condenses out and mixes with compressor oil. You can't dump that down the drain — it's an EPA violation. An OWS separates the oil from the water so the water can be legally discharged.

Drains

Automatic condensate drains remove accumulated moisture from the tank, dryer, and filter bowls. Most shops have manual drains that someone forgets to open. An automatic drain ensures moisture doesn't sit in the system and cause rust or contamination.

When a prospect mentions problems with moisture in their lines or tools, ask about their drains. It's often the cheapest fix in the entire system and it makes you look like an expert.

4. CFM & PSI Cheat Guide

Key Measurements Explained

CFM (Cubic Feet per Minute)

How much air the compressor delivers. This is the #1 sizing metric.

Think of it like water flow from a hose — CFM is how many gallons per minute you're getting.

PSI (Pounds per Square Inch)

How hard the air pushes. Most industrial applications need 90-125 PSI.

Think of it like water pressure — PSI is how forcefully the water comes out.

HP (Horsepower)

How powerful the motor is. More HP generally means more CFM, but it's not a perfect correlation.

Don't sell by HP alone. Always size by CFM demand.

Dew Point

The temperature at which moisture condenses out of compressed air. Lower dew point = drier air.

38°F = refrigerated dryer. -40°F = desiccant dryer.

Quick Sizing Formula

Step 1: Calculate Total CFM Demand
Total CFM = Sum of all machine CFM requirements
Step 2: Add Headroom (Safety Factor)
Required Compressor CFM = Total CFM × 1.25
25% headroom accounts for leaks, future growth, and peak demand
Step 3: Pick the Next Size Up
Always round UP to the next available compressor model

Worked Example

Scenario: Mike Garza’s Shop

Equipment: 3x Haas VF-2 + 1x Mazak QTN-200

  • Haas VF-2: ~8 CFM each → 3 × 8 = 24 CFM
  • Mazak QTN-200: ~6 CFM → 6 CFM
  • Misc. pneumatic tools, blow guns: ~8 CFM

Total demand: 38 CFM

With 25% headroom: 38 × 1.25 = 47.5 CFM

Recommendation: JRS-10E at 42 CFM is tight but workable for current setup. If they're truly adding 2 more machines, revisit then — don't oversell now.

Important — Don’t Rely on Machine Count Alone

Alex made this crystal clear: “You can have a shop with 2 machines and another with 12, and they have the same flow requirements.”

Machine count gets you in the ballpark. The Live Widget does the real work — it pulls actual specs from manufacturer databases when the prospect names their equipment. Your job is to get them talking about their machines.

OWS Sizing

OWS Formula
Required OWS CFM = Total Compressor CFM × 1.1
Always round UP to the next OWS model

Example: If you sell a JRS-10E (42 CFM), the OWS75 covers it. If you sell a JRS-25E (100 CFM), the OWS150 covers it.

Dryer Sizing

Simple rule: match the dryer CFM to the compressor CFM. If the compressor outputs 42 CFM, use a 50 CFM dryer. Account for altitude and extreme heat (Arizona summer, for example) — those reduce dryer efficiency. When in doubt, size up one model.

5. CNC Certifications by Industry

Certifications are the single most important intelligence signal in our system. When a CNC shop has a certification, they have to go through regular audits. Audits mean paperwork, inspections, and — increasingly — questions about their air system. That's our opening.

Priority: Aerospace (AS9100)

AS9100 — The Big One

What it is: Quality management standard for aerospace, defense, and space organizations. It's based on ISO 9001 but adds aerospace-specific requirements.

Who has it: Any shop making parts for Boeing, Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman, Raytheon, or any aerospace prime contractor.

Why it matters to us: AS9100 auditors are increasingly asking about air quality documentation. If a shop can't show their compressed air meets spec, they risk their certification — and losing that cert means losing their biggest contracts.

Renewal cycle: Every 3 years with annual surveillance audits.

Our data source: The OASIS database tracks AS9100 certifications and expiration dates. Our signal engine pulls from this directly.

How to Use This on a Call

“I noticed your AS9100 renewal is coming up in October. How's your air system documentation looking for the audit?”

This is the money opener. It shows you did your homework, and it connects your product to something they're already worried about. Most reps call and say “do you need a compressor?” — you're calling and talking about their certification.
